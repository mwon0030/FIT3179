NBA Data Visualisation

This visualisation shows the location of each shot a player took throughout the 2015-16 regular season, which means finals and all-star games are excluded from the dataset. Initially, the chart will be blank as no player has been selected yet. Upon selecting a player, all the shot locations of that player will loaded onto the chart. The legend on the top right shows which colour denotes a scored or missed shot. The longest shot attempted is annotated for each player. Hovering over each point also provides more information about the shot, such as the action that was used to take the shot, what type of shot it was, and the shot zone.

The differences between each player's shot chart generally reflect the position they play. Point guards and shooting guards such as Stephen Curry and Danny Green spend a lot of their time outside of the three-point arc (the large arc line), which is why many of their shots are behind the three-point line. Power forwards and centers such as Blake Griffin and Rudy Gobert play mostly inside the three-point arc and the key (the rectangle inside the three-point line), which is reflected in their respective shot charts. Not only can positional differences be observed, but also each player's play style, for example with Lebron James, most of his shots are taken on the left side of the court.

This visualisation shows how a team's offence and defence has changed throughout the first 30 games of the season. The calculation for offensive and defensive rating is complicated, however they essentially represent how efficient a team's offence or defence is, where a higher rating is better. The lines show the progression of each team throughout the first 30 games, and the points represent where each team is offensively and defensively rated after each game they have played. The rating values shown are a 10 game moving average as this is a better way to visualise how a team is tracking, since all teams will experience a large variance in performance throughout a long 82 game season. The legend on the top right denotes which colour is associated with which team. The drop down list on the bottom can be used to display all teams or one team at a time. Hovering over a point will reveal which game number the team has just played. The annotations highlight the first game and the 30th game played.

From the three teams shown, each team has improved in some capacity from where they started. The Cavaliers improved by 7 offensive rating points while their defensive rating has dropped 2 points. The Rockets have significantly improved their offence by 22 points however their defence has receded by 5 points. The Warriors are the only team that improved in both offence and defence by gaining 8 and 3 points respectively. Both the Cavaliers and the Rockets experienced large improvements early on in the season, however the Cavaliers started to drop off after game 15 and the Rockets began to decline after game 25.

This visualisation shows how skilled a player is in one aspect of the game in comparison to other players. The radar line chart makes it easy to compare many categories at once by placing each category equidistantly. The larger the area of the radar, the more well rounded a player is, and the closer the tip of the radar is to the labelled category, the better the player is in that statistic. The statistics shown are points (PTS), rebounds (REB), assists (AST), steals (STL) and blocks (BLK). Points are the points gained by scoring a shot, rebounds are acquired by being the first to possess the ball after a missed shot, assists are given when a teammate scores directly after they were passed the ball, steals are when a player takes possession of the ball off an opponent and blocks are given when a player blocks an opponents' shot while they are in their shooting motion. The numbers annotated on the graph are the average number of that statistic a player has accumulated per game. This average is taken over the entire regular season. The legend shows which colour is associated with which player.

The differences between the two players can be explained by their difference of position and physique. Russell Westbrook is a point guard who is responsible for distributing the ball to their teammates. Typically this means they have more possession of the ball which allows them to score more points as well as pass the ball to teammates to give them the opportunity to score leading to more assists. Rudy Gobert is a center who is responsible for rebounding the ball and protecting close range shots. They are generally the tallest players in the team which makes it easier for them to rebound the ball as well as block their opponents' shots.

This visualisation shows the distribution of the height and weight of active NBA players. The weight is measured in kilograms (KG) while the height is measured in centimeters (CM). The legend shows a color scale that represents the number of players for each height and weight range. Hovering over a rectangle on the chart will reveal the exact number of players for the given height and weight range.

From the visualisation, a majority of the players are within 190-215 centimeters tall and 85-115 kilograms heavy. The highest number of players within a height and weight range has 44 players within 195-200CM and 95-100KG. The tallest and heaviest active NBA player is in a class of his own standing between 225-230CM tall and weighing 140-145KG.
